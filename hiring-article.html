Hiring Your First 10 Employees

Updated December 2024 12 min read Talent & Hiring

Your first 10 hires will define your company's culture, set the pace for execution, and determine whether you achieve product-market fit. Yet as an early-stage startup, you're competing against established companies with bigger budgets, stronger brands, and more resources. Here's how to win the talent war anyway.

Why Your First 10 Hires Matter More Than You Think

Research shows that early employees have an outsized impact on startup success. According to First Round Capital's analysis of their portfolio, the quality of a startup's first 10 hires is one of the strongest predictors of long-term success.

These early team members will:

  • Set the bar for performance and work ethic
  • Establish your company culture and values
  • Become your future leadership team
  • Determine your execution velocity and quality
  • Influence every subsequent hire through their network

Get these hires right, and you create a flywheel of talent acquisition. Get them wrong, and you'll spend years recovering from the cultural and operational debt.

The Ideal First 10: A Hiring Roadmap

While every startup's needs differ based on industry and business model, here's a proven sequence for building your first team:

1-2

Technical Co-founder / Lead Engineer

Someone who can build your MVP and owns the product architecture. Ideally a co-founder, but if hiring, offer significant equity (1-3%). Look for full-stack capability and startup experience.

3

First Sales/Business Development Hire

Once you have a working product, you need revenue. This person should have deep domain expertise in your target market and existing relationships. Expect to pay 50-70% of market rate plus aggressive commission structure.

4-5

Additional Engineers

As customer demand grows, add 1-2 more engineers. Focus on complementary skills (e.g., backend + frontend, or infrastructure + features). Cultural fit is critical—they'll help define your engineering culture.

6

Product Manager or Product-Focused Designer

With multiple engineers and growing customer feedback, you need someone dedicated to product strategy and prioritization. Early-stage PMs should be hands-on and technical.

7-8

Customer Success / Account Manager

As your customer base grows (10-20 paying customers), you need dedicated account management. This role prevents churn, drives expansion revenue, and provides critical product feedback. Make this hire before your founders burn out on support.

9

Marketing Lead or Growth Hacker

Once you've proven you can close deals through founder-led sales, it's time to scale acquisition. This hire should be T-shaped: broad marketing knowledge with deep expertise in one channel (content, performance marketing, or community).

10

Operations / Finance Generalist

With ~10 people and meaningful revenue, you need someone managing operations, finance, HR, and legal. This "Chief of Staff" role keeps founders focused on product and growth instead of administrative tasks. Bonus if they have fundraising experience.

Should You Hire a Recruiter?

Not yet. For your first 10 hires, founders should drive recruiting directly. These hires are too important to outsource, and the recruiting process teaches you critical skills about selling your vision, assessing talent, and building culture. Save the recruiting fees for later-stage hiring (employees 20+).

How to Compete When You Can't Pay Market Rate

Early-stage startups typically can't match big tech salaries. Here's how to win talent anyway:

1. Offer Meaningful Equity

Your first 10 employees should receive equity grants in these ranges:

  • Hires 1-3 (critical early roles): 0.5% - 2.0%
  • Hires 4-7 (senior individual contributors): 0.25% - 0.75%
  • Hires 8-10 (still early but less critical): 0.1% - 0.5%

Make equity real: Don't just hand out shares—explain what they could be worth. Show scenarios: "If we exit at $100M (comparable to Company X in our space), your 0.5% could be worth $500K. At $500M (our long-term goal), that's $2.5M."

2. Sell the Learning Opportunity

Early employees at startups learn faster than anywhere else. At a 10-person company, an engineer will touch the full stack, talk to customers directly, influence product strategy, and see the impact of their work immediately. At a 1,000-person company, they'll spend months on onboarding and quarters on a single feature.

Position the role as:

  • "5 years of experience in 2 years"
  • Direct exposure to founders and decision-making
  • Ownership over critical systems and processes
  • Opportunity to build something from scratch

3. Highlight Impact and Autonomy

Top performers want to see their work matter. At early-stage startups, every contribution directly impacts the company's trajectory. Compare this to big companies where individual contributions get lost in bureaucracy.

Sell the autonomy:

  • "You'll own the entire customer success function"
  • "Your code will serve 100% of our users"
  • "You'll define our product roadmap, not execute someone else's"

4. Leverage Your Mission

People want to work on problems that matter. If your company is solving a real problem in healthcare, education, climate, or other impactful areas, sell the mission hard.

Even for less "mission-driven" companies, find the compelling narrative. B2B SaaS might be "helping small businesses compete with enterprises" or "saving customers 10 hours per week."

5. Offer Flexibility and Culture

Startups can move faster than big companies on benefits that don't cost much but matter a lot:

  • Remote work flexibility (if it fits your model)
  • Flexible hours (results over face time)
  • Unlimited vacation (but model taking time off yourself)
  • Generous parental leave
  • Learning budgets for courses, books, conferences
  • Latest equipment and tools

Where to Find Great Early Employees

1. Your Network (50% of hires)

Your best early hires will come from people you've worked with before or who come highly recommended by people you trust. This is why experienced founders have an easier time recruiting—they've built networks of talented people over their careers.

Action steps:

  • List every talented person you've worked with in the past 10 years
  • Reach out personally with a specific ask: "I'm hiring a senior engineer. Who's the best engineer you've worked with that might be open to startup life?"
  • Offer referral bonuses to early employees ($5-10K for successful hires)

2. University Talent (20% of hires)

In Canada, leverage proximity to University of Waterloo, UBC, McGill, or other top schools. Recent grads and co-op students offer high potential at lower cost.

Target these groups:

  • Senior students in final year (hire before graduation)
  • Master's students looking for industry experience
  • Former Big Tech interns who want more ownership
  • Co-op students from top programs (Waterloo's CS/Engineering)

3. Competitor Companies and Adjacent Industries (20% of hires)

Identify companies solving similar problems or serving similar customers. Their employees already understand your market and customers.

How to poach (ethically):

  • Attend industry conferences and meetups
  • Engage with people writing blog posts or speaking about your space
  • Offer them something they can't get at current company: ownership, autonomy, or upside
  • Be respectful—don't badmouth their current employer

4. Job Boards and Platforms (10% of hires)

For early-stage startups, LinkedIn and Indeed will generate lots of applications but few great candidates. Better platforms for startup hiring:

  • AngelList Talent: Startup-focused candidates
  • Y Combinator Work at a Startup: High-quality, vetted candidates
  • Hired / Vettery: Pre-screened tech candidates
  • Local startup job boards: BetaKit Jobs (Canada), VanHack (Vancouver)
  • Community Slack channels: Tech Twitter, Product Hunt, indie hacker communities

The Interview Process: Fast but Rigorous

Early-stage startups should move quickly—great candidates get multiple offers. But speed shouldn't mean lowering the bar. Here's a proven process:

Stage 1: Phone Screen (30 minutes)

  • Assess basic qualifications and mutual interest
  • Explain the role, company stage, and expectations honestly
  • Gauge culture fit and communication skills
  • Disqualify obvious mismatches early

Stage 2: Technical/Skills Assessment (1-2 hours)

  • Engineers: Take-home project or pairing session (avoid whiteboard interviews)
  • Sales: Mock pitch or objection handling exercise
  • Product/Design: Product critique or design challenge
  • Marketing: Strategy presentation or campaign analysis

Pay candidates for take-home work exceeding 2 hours ($100-200).

Stage 3: Team Interviews (2-3 hours)

  • Meet 3-4 team members in different contexts
  • Assess culture fit, collaboration skills, and communication
  • Discuss real challenges they'd face in the role
  • Give candidate time to ask hard questions

Stage 4: Founder Interview & Reference Checks

  • Founder assesses strategic thinking and long-term fit
  • Conduct 2-3 back-channel references (people not on their list)
  • Ask references: "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate this person? What would it take to make them a 10?"

Total timeline: 1-2 weeks from first call to offer.

Making the Offer: Close Fast and Compellingly

Once you've decided to hire someone, move quickly:

  1. Call them the same day: "We want to move forward with an offer. Are you still interested?"
  2. Send written offer within 24 hours: Include salary, equity, benefits, and start date
  3. Explain equity in detail: Share cap table summary, vesting schedule, and exit scenarios
  4. Address concerns directly: If they're hesitant about salary, emphasize equity + learning
  5. Set a deadline: "We'd love to have your answer by Friday"—urgency prevents endless deliberation

The "Sell After the Offer" Call

After sending the offer, schedule a call where the candidate can ask anything. Bring in early employees to share their experience. This is your chance to address cold feet and reinforce why this opportunity is special.

Common Hiring Mistakes to Avoid

1. Hiring for Skills Over Values

The mistake: Hiring the most technically skilled candidate who doesn't align with your culture.

The fix: Establish your core values early (e.g., "customer obsession," "bias for action," "radical transparency"). Assess every candidate against these values explicitly. A great engineer who's a cultural misfit will poison your team.

2. Rushing to Fill Roles

The mistake: Hiring someone mediocre because you're desperate for help.

The fix: It's better to wait 2 extra months for the right person than hire the wrong person and spend 6 months recovering. A-players attract A-players. B-players hire C-players.

3. Underselling the Opportunity

The mistake: Being overly humble about your startup's potential.

The fix: Top candidates want to work on ambitious, important problems. Sell the vision: "We're going to be the [Stripe/Shopify/Slack] of [your industry]. You'll be employee #5 helping build that." Confidence is contagious.

4. Ignoring Red Flags

The mistake: Overlooking concerning signals because you like the candidate otherwise.

The fix: Trust your gut. If something feels off—they badmouth previous employers, make unrealistic demands, or show entitlement—don't ignore it. Early employees set the culture. One toxic hire can destroy team morale.

Onboarding: Setting Your First Hires Up for Success

Hiring is only half the battle. Great onboarding ensures new employees ramp quickly and feel invested in your success.

Before Day 1:

  • Send equipment and accounts setup instructions
  • Share reading materials: pitch deck, product docs, customer case studies
  • Assign an onboarding buddy (ideally your first hire)
  • Schedule 1:1s with every team member for their first week

Week 1:

  • Review company vision, values, and culture
  • Deep dive on product and target customers
  • Ship something small (fix a bug, write a blog post, close a deal)
  • Shadow a customer call or sales demo

Month 1:

  • Own a meaningful project with measurable impact
  • Establish 30/60/90 day goals together
  • Weekly 1:1s with manager (at this stage, probably a founder)
  • Request feedback: "What's been confusing? What should we improve?"

Key Takeaways

  • Your first 10 hires define your culture and determine startup success
  • Hire in sequence: technical co-founder, sales, engineers, PM, customer success, marketing, operations
  • Compete on equity, learning, impact, and mission—not just salary
  • Leverage your network for 50% of hires; it's your highest-quality source
  • Move fast but maintain high standards—never compromise on culture fit
  • Invest in onboarding; great hires can fail without proper support
  • Founders should drive recruiting directly for the first 10 hires
